As some of you may know, computer science is a field with a disproportionately small number of women as opposed to other disciplines. However, it wasn't always this way. During the early days of computing, women were dominating in software development. Then where does today's discrimination come from?

### History of Women in Computer Science

The history of computer science cannot be separated from women's involvement in it. Let's take for example Ada Lovelace, who is widely considered to have created the first ever computer program, Note G. She worked alongside Charles Babbage, the inventor of the modern computer's first ancestor in the early 19th century. Her contributions to it greatly outrank those of Babbage – in the end, she wrote <span class="bold">three times more notes</span> about the Difference Engine than he did.

Starting from the end of the 19th century, computers began being used to automate complex calculations. However, those machines couldn't just calculate things on their own, as they required a human operator to input the calculations. This job was almost exclusively done by women. They would work long hours, for salaries often lower than those of factory workers and without unions.

Software development, though the term didn't exist at the time, wasn't a science, and many early day programmers had no academic training. The domain was dominated by women, but it didn't have much credibility in the public eye. Hardware, on the other hand, was considered the "real man's job" and was thought of as a real science.

Throughout both World Wars, and especially during WWII, women were employed by the government to work as human computers for things like calculating ballistic trajectories. In fact, the team that cracked the German Enigma Code durin WWII was made up of <span class="bold">80% of women</span>!

Another example of women's involvement is the ENIAC six. They're a group of six women who came up with much of the logic behind the ENIAC, the first ever general-purpose computer. Many of the programming concepts they came up with are widely used to this day, like subroutines and nesting.

Unsurprisingly, the team working on the 1960s Apollo missions also had many women behind the scences, like Margaret Hamilton, who <span class="bold">single-handedly programmed the flight software used aboard the Apollo aircrafts</span>. Women were also involved in the manufacturing of hardware. Companies producing the computers for NASA employed skilled Navajo seamstresses that would painstakingly thread tiny copper wires through minuscule magnetic rings for the memory storage systems.

Despite all these examples of brilliant women, things weren't perfect for women in early computing. They would still be discriminated against on the basis of gender or race. Many women held job titles that were hierarchically beneath those of men despite similar workloads, so they would get paid less even after equal pay bills were passed. Plus, when women would make groundbreaking discoveries, those would often get misattributed to the men they worked with.

### The Shift to a Male-Dominated View

Once computers became more popular, a slow change began. The idea emerged that writing code requires some kind of natural brilliance or innate logic. Over the course of the 1960s and 1970s, software development began its shift in the public conscience towards an intellectual activity.

Since computer science began being thought of as a "real" science, it became masculinized. It was no longer work that required stereotypically feminine qualities, like precision and patience. Instead, it became a field where bright young men could show of their "natural brilliance". Women were pushed even more to the sidelines, and the 1980s saw a drop in female enrolment to computer science programs in universities.

<span class="bold">The stereotype of the computer nerd emerged.</span> A man, often white and from a middle-class background, sits in front of a computer all day in his dedication to the science. He's considered a sort of lone genius with some kind of innate brilliance. This stereotype can be thought of as a precursor to Sheldon Cooper-type characters, or someone who could be pejoratively compares to modern-day Redditors.

Women who already worked with computers would get men as trainees. Once those men would reach a certain level, they would get promoted to managerial positions, and women would remain in the role of assistant or secretary despite their superior experience and skillset.

The stereotype began reinforcing itself. Men would get encouraged to study computer science. Women would get pushed away from it, whether from internal or external bias.

### Where are we now?

As of the 2023-2024 school year, in Canada, only <span class="bold">28.5% of the people studying computer science are women</span>. How come?

As we’ve seen previously, we’re in a field that values innate brilliance as a predictor of individual success. However, research has shown that fields that value this kind of “raw talent” factor tend to have significantly fewer women and people of color in them.

That’s because there are widespread cultural associations linking the stereotype of raw intelligence to white men, as exemplified by the archetype of the computer nerd. Consequently, women and black people are stereotyped as lacking this talent. Some of the people belonging to those groups are implicitly discouraged from joining fields where raw intelligence is so highly valued, either because of internalized bias or external influence.

While not all women or people of color may face this opposition, many people in these marginalized groups do experience it without even realizing it. If you see yourself in what’s being described, know that you are not alone.

If you are reading this article as a non-marginalized person, it may be beneficial to think about your impact on inclusivity in the computer science field. It matters when you help your fellow classmates, because a supportive climate leads to discovery and innovation. We should all strive to avoid the idea of natural brilliance when talking about computer science, because after all, there is really no such thing.

### Conclusions

Though women have been integral to the development of computer science since the beginning of the discipline, their involvement has been minimized or ignored. We can honor their contributions now despite the fact that many of their names were lost to history.

Computer science has evolved to see software development as a masculine discipline, a mindset which to this day still discourages women or people of color from joining the field. We have to stop valuing “natural brilliance” so much, as <span class="bold">it is not a true predictor of success in computer science</span>. It is possible to get knowledgeable in any domain of programming without being a Sheldon Cooper.

Despite what we may think, there is still rampant sexism in some domains of computer science. We must keep on working towards eliminating sexism entirely, and that starts with you, the student, questioning your own preconceived notions.
